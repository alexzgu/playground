13.1
Random Effects Models
Throughout most of this book we have assumed that the factors in an experiment were ﬁxed
factors, that is, the levels of the factors used by the experimenter were the specic levels of
interest. The implication of this, of course, is that the statistical inferences made about these
factors are conned to the specic levels studied. That is, if three material types are investigat-
ed as in the battery life experiment of Example 5.1, our conclusions are valid only about those
specic material types. A variation of this occurs when the factor or factors are quantitative.
In these situations, we often use a regression model relating the response to the factors to pre-
dict the response over the region spanned by the factor levels used in the experimental design.
Several examples of this were presented in Chapters 5 through 9. In general, with a xed
effect, we say that the inference space of the experiment is the specic set of factor levels
investigated.
