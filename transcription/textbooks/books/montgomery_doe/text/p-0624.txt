The practical implications of this experiment and the analysis are very important. The
objective of the experimenter is to nd the source of the variability in raw material purity. If
it results from differences among suppliers, we may be able to solve the problem by select-
ing the “best” supplier. However, that solution is not applicable here because the major source
of variability is the batch-to-batch purity variation within suppliers. Therefore, we must attack
the problem by working with the suppliers to reduce their batch-to-batch variability. This may
involve modications to the suppliers’production processes or their internal quality assurance
system.
Notice what would have happened if we had incorrectly analyzed this design as a two-
factor factorial experiment. If batches are considered to be crossed with suppliers, we obtain
batch totals of 2, 3, 2, and 16, with each batch suppliers cell containing three replicates.
Thus, a sum of squares due to batches and an interaction sum of squares can be computed. The
complete factorial analysis of variance is shown in Table 14.5, assuming the mixed model.
This analysis indicates that batches differ signicantly and that there is a signicant
interaction between batches and suppliers. However, it is difcult to give a practical interpre-
tation of the batches suppliers interaction. For example, does this signicant interaction
mean that the supplier effect is not constant from batch to batch? Furthermore, the signicant
interaction coupled with the nonsignicant supplier effect could lead the analyst to conclude
that suppliers really differ but their effect is masked by the signicant interaction.
